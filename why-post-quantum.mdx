## The Harvest Now, Decrypt Later Threat

Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL) is a data collection strategy used by well-resourced adversaries. The premise is straightforward: capture ciphertext today, store it cheaply, and decrypt it once a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) becomes powerful enough to break the underlying algorithms.

The algorithms at risk are the ones that protect virtually all internet traffic today: RSA, ECDH, ECDSA, and DHE. A CRQC running Shor's algorithm would break all of them. The current scientific consensus places the arrival of such a machine somewhere between 2029 and 2035, though some estimates fall earlier.

This means that sensitive data encrypted with RSA-2048 or P-256 today is not safe. Anyone who has stored that traffic can decrypt it later. For data with a long shelf life, such as health records, financial contracts, intellectual property, and government communications, the breach is effectively happening right now.

<Warning>
  If your organization transmits data that must remain confidential for more than five years, that data may already be compromised. HNDL attacks require no active intrusion. Passive collection of encrypted traffic is routine at scale. Waiting until quantum computers are available before acting is waiting too long.
</Warning>

## Regulatory and Industry Timeline

The post-quantum transition is not only a technology concern. Governments and standards bodies have set binding deadlines.

| Event | Date |
|---|---|
| NIST PQC standards finalized (FIPS 203, 204, 205) | August 2024 |
| CNSA 2.0 PQC adoption required for NSS | 2025 (in progress) |
| Major cloud providers offer PQC TLS | 2025 |
| NIS2 Directive enforcement begins (EU) | October 2024 |
| Estimated CRQC arrival window | 2029 to 2035 |

Organizations in regulated industries face dual pressure: the technical threat and the compliance mandate. Failing to demonstrate a post-quantum migration roadmap is increasingly a finding in security audits and regulatory examinations.

## Who Needs to Act

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Financial Services">
    Banks, payment processors, and capital markets firms handle transaction data and customer records with retention periods measured in decades. HNDL attacks targeting TLS-encrypted API traffic, inter-bank messaging, and card payment flows are realistic scenarios today.

    Regulators including the ECB, FFIEC, and FCA have issued guidance on quantum risk. DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) requires financial entities in the EU to assess and address cryptographic risk as part of ICT risk management. Firms with SWIFT connectivity face additional pressure as SWIFT has published its own PQC roadmap.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Healthcare">
    Patient records, genomic data, and clinical trial results are among the most sensitive long-lived datasets in existence. HIPAA does not currently mandate PQC, but the HHS Office for Civil Rights has signaled that algorithm agility is an expected component of a reasonable security posture.

    Healthcare organizations also face significant third-party exposure. Medical devices, EMR systems, and insurance data exchanges often rely on RSA certificates that cannot be patched quickly. Early inventory and planning is essential.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Government and Defense">
    CNSA 2.0 requires National Security Systems to transition to approved PQC algorithms on an accelerating schedule. Contractors and suppliers to US federal agencies are subject to the same requirements under DFARS and emerging OMB guidance.

    The UK NCSC, EU ENISA, and German BSI have all published migration guidance for government entities. Intelligence community standards are more stringent and move faster than public-sector requirements.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Technology Companies">
    SaaS platforms, cloud infrastructure providers, and software vendors face a different kind of pressure: their customers. Enterprise buyers are increasingly including PQC readiness in vendor security questionnaires. Failure to demonstrate a credible migration plan is becoming a procurement blocker.

    Technology companies also tend to have the most complex cryptographic estates: long-lived service accounts, custom TLS termination, internally issued certificates, and sprawling microservice meshes. The discovery problem alone justifies early action.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
